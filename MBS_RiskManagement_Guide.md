## 1. What Is an MBS and Why Does It Matter

### The Asset Class

A **Mortgage-Backed Security (MBS)** is a bond whose cash flows are generated by a pool of residential mortgages. Each month, thousands of borrowers make their mortgage payments — interest plus scheduled principal plus any unscheduled early repayments — and those cash flows pass through to the MBS investor (hence the term **pass-through security**).

Agency MBS (issued or guaranteed by Freddie Mac, Fannie Mae, or Ginnie Mae) represent over **$12 trillion** in outstanding notional as of 2024, making them the largest segment of the US fixed-income market, larger than Treasuries. They are held by the Fed, commercial banks, insurance companies, and foreign central banks. Understanding MBS is not optional for anyone in rates, structured credit, or risk management.

### Why MBS Are Hard to Price

A standard bond has fixed, contractual cash flows and a clean closed-form price:
$$P = \sum_{t=1}^{T} \frac{CF_t}{(1+y)^t}$$

An MBS does not. The borrower holds two **embedded American options**:

| Option | Borrower action | Investor's position |
|---|---|---|
| **Call (prepayment)** | Pay off the loan early when rates fall and refinancing is cheaper | Short a call on rates |
| **Put (default)** | Stop paying and walk away when the house is worth less than the loan | Short a put on house prices |

Because both options depend on the path of interest rates and property values over the loan's life, the cash flows are **path-dependent** and **stochastic**. No closed-form solution exists. You need simulation.

This is why MBS sit at the intersection of credit risk, prepayment modeling, and interest-rate derivatives — and why they appear on derivatives-focused resumes.

### What This Project Builds

A loan-level MBS risk framework using real Freddie Mac data, covering all three sources of uncertainty:

1. **Default risk** — When will a borrower stop paying?
2. **Prepayment risk** — When will a borrower pay off early?
3. **Interest rate risk** — How does the yield curve evolve, and how does it drive both of the above?

The interest rate layer (Vasicek model + Monte Carlo simulation) is the extension that closes the loop and makes the system capable of pricing, OAS computation, and rate-shock stress testing.

## 2. The Three Risk Pillars

### 2.1 Cash Flow Mechanics (First Principles)

For a single **fixed-rate mortgage (FRM)** with original balance $B_0$, annual coupon rate $c$, and term $N$ months:

**Level monthly payment** (standard annuity formula):
$$MP = B_0 \cdot \frac{(c/12)(1+c/12)^N}{(1+c/12)^N - 1}$$

In each month $t$, given surviving balance $B_{t-1}$:

$$\text{Interest}_t = B_{t-1} \cdot \frac{c}{12}$$
$$\text{Scheduled Principal}_t = MP - \text{Interest}_t$$
$$\text{Prepayment}_t = (B_{t-1} - \text{Scheduled Principal}_t) \cdot \text{SMM}_t$$
$$B_t = B_{t-1} - \text{Scheduled Principal}_t - \text{Prepayment}_t$$

where **SMM** (Single Monthly Mortality) is the fraction of the surviving balance that prepays unscheduled in month $t$.

**SMM ↔ CPR relationship** (CPR = Conditional Prepayment Rate, the annualized version):
$$\text{CPR} = 1 - (1 - \text{SMM})^{12}, \qquad \text{SMM} = 1 - (1-\text{CPR})^{1/12}$$

The pool-level cash flow in month $t$ is:
$$CF_t = \sum_{i \in \text{pool}} \left[\text{Interest}_{i,t} + \text{Scheduled Principal}_{i,t} + \text{Prepayment}_{i,t} + \text{Recovery}_{i,t} \cdot \mathbf{1}_{\{\text{default}_i\}}\right]$$

### 2.2 Pillar Map

| Pillar | Question answered | Model | Implemented? |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Credit / Default** | Will this borrower stop paying? | Logistic Regression, Cox Proportional Hazards | ✅ Yes |
| **Prepayment** | Will this borrower pay off early? | PSA benchmark, rate-dependent CPR | ✅ Yes (static PSA); ⚠️ Rate-coupling to add |
| **Interest Rate** | How does the short rate evolve? | Vasicek (Ornstein-Uhlenbeck) | ❌ Missing — build this |

The three pillars are not independent. The **refinancing incentive** (borrower coupon minus current market rate) drives prepayment and depends on interest rates. **Negative equity** (property value below loan balance) drives strategic default and correlates with rate levels through LTV. A complete model must couple all three through a common rate process.

df['default'] = (pd.to_numeric(df['delinquency_status'], errors='coerce').fillna(0) > 0).astype(int)

Use the **strict definition** for modeling default risk. The conservative definition conflates 30-day delinquency (which often cures) with actual credit loss.

### 4.6 Data Window Rationale — Why Each Model Uses a Different Time Range

This is an intentional design decision, not an inconsistency. Each model uses the data window that best serves its statistical purpose. The three windows do not need to match, and mixing them carelessly would actually hurt model quality.

All Freddie Mac data comes from the **2018–2025 download** (8 annual files). Each model then filters to the sub-window that best serves its statistical purpose. This is an intentional design decision, not an inconsistency — using the wrong window for a model would hurt its quality, not help it.

| Model | Data window | Source | Why this window |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Logistic Regression** | 2021–2025 | `panel_logistic_2021_2025.parquet` | Captures three distinct macro regimes (zero-rate era, hiking cycle, plateau) in tight succession, making the macro features (UNRATE, MORTGAGE30US, HPI) highly informative. A static snapshot model — doesn't need to observe *when* default happens, only *whether* it happened. |
| **Cox PH Survival Model** | 2018–2025 | `panel_cph_2018_2025.parquet` | Needs to observe reasonably complete default lifecycles. Loans originated in 2018 are now 7 years old — old enough to have gone through a full default cycle (the default hazard peaks in months 24–60). The 2020 COVID cohort adds a stress-period cluster of observed events. Using only 2021+ would give almost no resolved defaults to train on since recent loans are still performing or in early delinquency. |
| **PSA Prepayment Model** | 2021–2025 | `panel_logistic_2021_2025.parquet` (reuse) | The 2021 refinancing boom and the 2022 collapse in CPR are precisely the rate-variation needed to characterize prepayment dynamics. The richest window available for this purpose. |
| **Vasicek Calibration** | 2000–2025 | FRED only — no Freddie Mac data | Independent of Freddie Mac entirely. The longer the rate history, the better the OLS estimates of mean-reversion speed and long-run level. Uses FRED `GS1` series. |

**Why 2018 is sufficient for Cox PH (and 2014 is not necessary):** The mortgage default hazard is hump-shaped over loan age — it rises steeply in the first 2–3 years, peaks around year 4–5, and then declines as surviving loans are increasingly high-quality. Loans originated in 2018 have aged through this entire peak by 2025. Adding 2014–2017 would give more data but the marginal statistical gain does not justify the extra ~15–20 GB of disk space. The 2018 decision is a deliberate tradeoff between statistical completeness and practical constraints.

**Critical rule for Claude Code:** Apply date filters immediately after loading the merged panel — before any feature engineering, joining, or modeling. Each notebook loads its own pre-filtered parquet file. Never cross-load parquet files between notebooks (e.g., do not load `panel_cph_2018_2025.parquet` in the logistic regression notebook).

**How the models interact in the final pricer:** The logistic regression produces a loan-level PD (probability of default) used in the cash flow generator. The Cox PH model produces a time-varying hazard function for alternative use in competing-risks scenarios. Both are trained on their own windows and scored on the same future pool at inference time — no data leakage because neither model sees test-period data during training.

## 5. Module A — Default Risk

### 5.1 The Problem

We observe a panel of $N$ loans, each with up to $T$ monthly observations. Define:
- $Y_i \in \{0,1\}$: whether loan $i$ ever defaults (for logistic regression)
- $(T_i, \delta_i)$: duration and event indicator for loan $i$ (for survival analysis)
  - $T_i$ = Loan Age at last observation
  - $\delta_i = 1$ if the loan defaulted, $0$ if it is right-censored (still performing)

Two models are implemented, with complementary strengths.

---

### 5.2 Model A1 — Logistic Regression

**Statistical model:**
$$P(\text{Default}_i = 1 \mid \mathbf{x}_i) = \sigma(\mathbf{x}_i^\top \boldsymbol{\beta}) = \frac{1}{1 + e^{-\mathbf{x}_i^\top \boldsymbol{\beta}}}$$

The log-likelihood is:
$$\ell(\boldsymbol{\beta}) = \sum_{i=1}^N \left[Y_i \log \sigma(\mathbf{x}_i^\top\boldsymbol{\beta}) + (1-Y_i)\log(1-\sigma(\mathbf{x}_i^\top\boldsymbol{\beta}))\right]$$

**Data format:** Collapse the monthly panel to one row per loan (loan-level). The target is whether the loan ever reached 90+ DPD or a terminal zero-balance code.

**Feature set:**

| Feature | Construction | Economic rationale |
|---|---|---|
| `credit_score` | Clipped to [300, 850] | Primary predictor of willingness to pay |
| `ltv` | Original LTV, clipped at 150 | Collateral coverage; recovery on default |
| `dti` | Original DTI; `999` → `NaN` | Payment burden at origination |
| `orig_interest_rate` | Raw note rate | Level of fixed payment obligation |
| `months_remaining_ratio` | `orig_loan_term - loan_age` | Loan seasoning |
| `loan_age_yrs` | `loan_age / 12` | Seasoning in years |
| `high_dti` | `dti > 43` | Binary; CFPB Qualified Mortgage threshold |
| `high_ltv` | `ltv > 80` | Binary; PMI threshold |
| `low_fico` | `credit_score < 660` | Binary; subprime threshold |
| `in_negative_equity` | `ltv > 100` | Binary; default put option is in-the-money |
| `mortgage_rate_orig` | `MORTGAGE30US` at origination quarter | Rate environment borrower locked into |
| `unemp_rate_orig` | `UNRATE` at origination quarter | Labor market stress at origination |
| `hpi_yoy_orig` | `SPCS20RSA` YoY at origination | Housing appreciation momentum |

**Class imbalance:** The base default rate is approximately 0.3% in 2024 data. Two approaches:

- `class_weight='balanced'` in sklearn's `LogisticRegression` — re-weights the cross-entropy loss by $N / (2 \cdot N_{\text{class}})$ for each class. Simple, no resampling needed.
- Stratified reservoir sampling to create a balanced training set (keep all defaults, reservoir-sample non-defaults).

**Calibration:** After fitting on training data, apply Platt scaling:
```python

calibrated_model.fit(X_valid, y_valid)
```
This maps raw log-odds to well-calibrated probabilities — essential for using the model's output as a Probability of Default (PD) in expected loss calculations.

**Solver recommendation:** `solver='lbfgs'` for small datasets; `solver='saga'` for large datasets (> 500K rows) with sparse features.

### 5.3 Model A2 — Cox Proportional Hazards

**Why CPH is more correct than logistic regression here:**

A loan still performing at the end of 2024 is not a confirmed non-default — it is **right-censored**: we know it has not defaulted *yet* but not whether it will. Logistic regression ignores this and treats censored loans as true zeros, underestimating default probability for young loans who haven't had time to default. The Cox model handles censoring correctly by conditioning on the set of loans still at risk at each event time.

**The hazard function:**
$$h(t \mid \mathbf{x}_i) = h_0(t) \cdot \exp(\mathbf{x}_i^\top \boldsymbol{\beta})$$

- $h(t \mid \mathbf{x}_i)$: instantaneous conditional default probability at loan age $t$, given covariates $\mathbf{x}_i$
- $h_0(t)$: baseline hazard — left completely unspecified (the semiparametric trick). It captures the age effect common to all loans.
- $\exp(\mathbf{x}_i^\top \boldsymbol{\beta})$: multiplicative covariate effect — constant over time (the proportional hazards assumption)

**Partial likelihood (Cox, 1972):** The baseline hazard cancels:
$$\ell(\boldsymbol{\beta}) = \sum_{i:\delta_i=1} \left[\mathbf{x}_i^\top \boldsymbol{\beta} - \log \sum_{j \in \mathcal{R}(T_i)} \exp(\mathbf{x}_j^\top \boldsymbol{\beta})\right]$$

where $\mathcal{R}(T_i) = \{j : T_j \geq T_i\}$ is the **risk set** — loans still at risk (neither defaulted nor censored) at the moment loan $i$ defaults.

**Survival function:**
$$S(t \mid \mathbf{x}_i) = S_0(t)^{\exp(\mathbf{x}_i^\top \boldsymbol{\beta})}$$

**Proportional hazards assumption test:** The effect of each covariate must be constant over loan age. Test with **Schoenfeld residuals**: for each default event, compute the difference between the defaulted loan's covariate value and the risk-set-weighted average. Under PH, these residuals should be uncorrelated with time. Use `lifelines`' `cph.check_assumptions()` which runs this test and plots the smoothed residuals vs. time automatically.

**Data format:** One row per loan. Required columns:
- `duration` (= Loan Age at last observation)
- `event` (= 1 if default, 0 if censored)
- All covariate columns

**Covariates for CPH:**

- **Loss Given Default (LGD):** The models predict PD only. For expected loss: $EL = PD \times LGD \times EAD$. Build an LGD sub-model: regress `actual_loss / orig_upb` on LTV, state, resolution type among defaulted loans.

## 6. Module B — Prepayment Risk

### 6.1 The PSA Benchmark

The **PSA (Public Securities Association) prepayment benchmark** is the industry standard quoting convention. **100% PSA** defines a CPR schedule:

$$\text{CPR}_{100\%\text{PSA}}(t) = \begin{cases} 0.2\% \times t & t < 30 \text{ months} \\ 6\% & t \geq 30 \text{ months} \end{cases}$$

The pool is said to prepay at "$k$% PSA" if its actual CPR is $k/100$ times this schedule at each month $t$. PSA 200 = twice the benchmark speed.

**Converting CPR to SMM (for cash flow simulation):**
$$\text{SMM}_t = 1 - (1 - \text{CPR}_t)^{1/12}$$

### 6.2 Computing Actual CPR from Freddie Mac Data

**For fixed-rate mortgages (FRM only):**

For each loan-month observation:
1. Compute the **scheduled monthly payment** using the annuity formula on the original balance and rate.
2. Compute the **scheduled principal** = payment − interest.
3. Compute the **actual unscheduled prepayment** = UPB decrease beyond scheduled principal.

Pool-level aggregation:
$$\text{SMM}_t^{\text{pool}} = \frac{\sum_i \text{Prepayment}_{i,t}}{\sum_i (B_{i,t-1} - \text{Scheduled Principal}_{i,t})}$$
$$\text{CPR}_t = 1 - (1 - \text{SMM}_t^{\text{pool}})^{12}$$

**Vectorized implementation:**
```python
def compute_scheduled_principal(df):
    """
    df must have columns: loan_id, loan_age, orig_upb, orig_interest_rate,
                          orig_loan_term, current_interest_rate,
                          interest_bearing_upb, zero_balance_code
    """
    df = df.sort_values(['loan_id', 'loan_age']).copy()

    # Monthly rate and remaining term
    mrate  = (df['current_interest_rate'] / 100) / 12
    rem    = (df['orig_loan_term'] - df['loan_age']).clip(lower=1)

    # Previous period balance (shift within loan)
    df['bal_prev'] = df.groupby('loan_id')['interest_bearing_upb'].shift(1)
    # For month 1, use orig_upb
    mask = df['loan_age'] == df.groupby('loan_id')['loan_age'].transform('min')
    df.loc[mask, 'bal_prev'] = df.loc[mask, 'orig_upb']

    bal = df['bal_prev'].values
    r   = mrate.values
    n   = rem.values

    # Level payment (vectorized)
    with np.errstate(divide='ignore', invalid='ignore'):
        pmt = np.where(
            r == 0,
            bal / n,
            bal * (r * (1 + r)**n) / ((1 + r)**n - 1)
        )
    interest      = bal * r
    sched_prin    = np.minimum(pmt - interest, bal)
    bal_after_sp  = bal - sched_prin
    prepay        = np.maximum(bal_after_sp - df['interest_bearing_upb'].values, 0)

    df['scheduled_principal'] = sched_prin
    df['prepayment']          = prepay
    return df


def pool_cpr(df):
    """Compute monthly pool-level CPR time series."""
    monthly = df.groupby('reporting_period').agg(
        beg_bal   = ('bal_prev', 'sum'),
        sched_p   = ('scheduled_principal', 'sum'),
        prepay    = ('prepayment', 'sum'),
    ).reset_index()

    denom = (monthly['beg_bal'] - monthly['sched_p']).clip(lower=1)
    monthly['smm'] = monthly['prepay'] / denom
    monthly['cpr'] = 1 - (1 - monthly['smm'])**12
    return monthly
```

### 6.3 PSA Speed Fitting

Given the observed monthly CPR series, find the scalar PSA multiplier $k$ that best fits:
$$\hat{k} = \frac{\sum_{t=1}^{T} \text{CPR}_t \cdot \text{CPR}_{100\%\text{PSA}}(t)}{\sum_{t=1}^{T} \text{CPR}_{100\%\text{PSA}}(t)^2}$$

This is a least-squares projection. The result tells you the pool prepays at "$100\hat{k}$% PSA".

```python
monthly['t'] = np.arange(1, len(monthly) + 1)
monthly['cpr_100psa'] = np.where(monthly['t'] < 30, 0.002 * monthly['t'], 0.06)

k = (monthly['cpr'] * monthly['cpr_100psa']).sum() / (monthly['cpr_100psa']**2).sum()
print(f"Pool equivalent PSA speed: {100*k:.1f}%")
```

### 6.4 Rate-Dependent CPR (Extension — Required for Module D)

Static PSA cannot answer how prepayments change across rate scenarios. For the Monte Carlo pricer, replace the scalar $k$ with a rate-dependent function of the **refinancing incentive**:

$$\text{RI}_t = c - r_t^{30Y}$$

where $c$ is the borrower's coupon and $r_t^{30Y}$ is the prevailing 30-year mortgage rate on the simulated rate path.

**Rate-dependent PSA multiplier** (logistic):
$$k(\text{RI}_t) = k_{\min} + (k_{\max} - k_{\min}) \cdot \sigma\!\left(\alpha + \beta \cdot \text{RI}_t\right)$$

Typical parameter values: $k_{\min} = 0.5$, $k_{\max} = 3.0$, $\beta = 8.0$, $\alpha$ shifts the inflection point. Calibrate $\alpha$ so that $k(0) \approx 1$ (when RI = 0, pool prepays at 100% PSA).

```python
def rate_dependent_psa_k(ri, k_min=0.5, k_max=3.0, alpha=-0.04, beta=8.0):
    """PSA speed multiplier as logistic function of refinancing incentive."""
    return k_min + (k_max - k_min) / (1 + np.exp(-(alpha + beta * ri)))

def compute_cpr_paths(mortgage_rate_paths, coupon, term):
    """
    mortgage_rate_paths: shape (n_paths, n_steps)
    Returns cpr_paths: shape (n_paths, n_steps)
    """
    n_paths, n_steps = mortgage_rate_paths.shape
    months = np.arange(1, n_steps + 1)
    psa_base = np.where(months < 30, 0.002 * months, 0.06)     # (n_steps,)
    ri       = coupon - mortgage_rate_paths                      # (n_paths, n_steps)
    k        = rate_dependent_psa_k(ri)                          # (n_paths, n_steps)
    cpr      = k * psa_base[np.newaxis, :]                       # (n_paths, n_steps)
    return np.clip(cpr, 0.0, 0.99)
```

**Burnout effect (optional enhancement):** Burnout captures that the most rate-sensitive borrowers refinance first. As the pool ages, the remaining borrowers are progressively less responsive. Model burnout as a dampening of $k$ proportional to cumulative prepayment experience:
$$k_{\text{burnout}}(t) = k(\text{RI}_t) \cdot \left(\frac{B_t}{B_0}\right)^{\gamma}$$
where $\gamma > 0$ (typically 0.3–0.5) and $B_t / B_0$ is the fraction of original pool balance remaining. As prepayments reduce the surviving balance, $k$ falls.

## 7. Module C — Interest Rate Model (Vasicek)

This module is the missing piece. It generates the stochastic interest rate paths that drive both the prepayment model and the discount factors used to price the MBS.

### 7.1 The Vasicek Model — Theory from First Principles

**The SDE (Ornstein-Uhlenbeck process):**
$$dr_t = \kappa(\theta - r_t)\,dt + \sigma\,dW_t$$

**Every object defined:**

| Symbol | Type | Meaning |
|---|---|---|
| $r_t$ | Random variable (for each $t$) | The short-term risk-free interest rate at time $t$. This is the *instantaneous* rate — the rate for an infinitesimally short borrowing period. |
| $\kappa > 0$ | Deterministic constant | Mean-reversion speed. Units: year$^{-1}$. Measures how fast $r_t$ is pulled back toward $\theta$. Half-life of rate deviation: $\ln(2)/\kappa$. |
| $\theta$ | Deterministic constant | Long-run mean of $r_t$. As $t \to \infty$, $\mathbb{E}[r_t] \to \theta$. |
| $\sigma > 0$ | Deterministic constant | Volatility of the short rate. Units: year$^{-1/2}$. |
| $W_t$ | Brownian motion (stochastic process) | Standard Wiener process. $W_t - W_s \sim \mathcal{N}(0, t-s)$ for $t > s$, independent increments. All randomness enters through $dW_t = W_{t+dt} - W_t \sim \mathcal{N}(0, dt)$. |

The term $\kappa(\theta - r_t)dt$ is the drift: it pulls $r_t$ toward $\theta$ at speed proportional to the current deviation $(\theta - r_t)$. The term $\sigma\,dW_t$ is the diffusion: it adds Gaussian noise at each instant.

**Exact solution (integrating the SDE with integrating factor $e^{\kappa t}$):**
$$r_t = r_0 e^{-\kappa t} + \theta(1 - e^{-\kappa t}) + \sigma \int_0^t e^{-\kappa(t-s)}\,dW_s$$

The stochastic integral is normally distributed (since $W$ has Gaussian increments and the integrand is deterministic), so:
$$r_t \mid r_0 \sim \mathcal{N}\!\left(\underbrace{r_0 e^{-\kappa t} + \theta(1-e^{-\kappa t})}_{\mathbb{E}[r_t|r_0]},\;\underbrace{\frac{\sigma^2(1-e^{-2\kappa t})}{2\kappa}}_{\text{Var}(r_t|r_0)}\right)$$

This is the **transition density** — it tells you exactly the distribution of the rate at any future time given today's rate.

**Long-run distribution** (as $t \to \infty$):
$$r_\infty \sim \mathcal{N}\!\left(\theta,\; \frac{\sigma^2}{2\kappa}\right)$$

The rate is stationary — it fluctuates in a band around $\theta$ with width proportional to $\sigma/\sqrt{2\kappa}$.

**Discrete Euler-Maruyama scheme** (exact for Vasicek; $\Delta t = 1/12$ year):
$$r_{t+\Delta t} = r_t\,e^{-\kappa\Delta t} + \theta(1-e^{-\kappa\Delta t}) + \sigma\sqrt{\frac{1-e^{-2\kappa\Delta t}}{2\kappa}}\,Z_t$$
$$Z_t \overset{\text{iid}}{\sim} \mathcal{N}(0,1)$$

Note this is the **exact** discretization (not an approximation), because the transition density is Gaussian.

### 7.2 Zero-Coupon Bond Price (Affine Term Structure)

A zero-coupon bond maturing at $T$ pays 1 at time $T$. Its price at time $t$ under Vasicek (risk-neutral measure $\mathbb{Q}$):

$$P(t,T) = A(t,T) \cdot e^{-B(t,T)\,r_t}$$

where $\tau = T - t$ is time to maturity and:
$$B(t,T) = \frac{1 - e^{-\kappa\tau}}{\kappa}$$
$$A(t,T) = \exp\!\left[\left(\theta - \frac{\sigma^2}{2\kappa^2}\right)(B - \tau) - \frac{\sigma^2}{4\kappa}\,B^2\right]$$

**The yield** (continuously compounded) for maturity $\tau$:
$$y(t,T) = -\frac{\ln P(t,T)}{\tau} = -\frac{\ln A}{\tau} + \frac{B}{\tau}\,r_t$$

This is **affine in $r_t$**: the yield is a linear function of the current short rate. This is the defining property of the **Affine Term Structure Model (ATSM)** class.

**Why this matters for MBS:** The bond pricing formula lets you convert the simulated short rate path to a 30-year yield at each point in time along each path, which you need to compute the refinancing incentive and discount cash flows.

---

### 7.3 Calibration — Time-Series MLE (Historical Measure $\mathbb{P}$)

Given monthly short-rate observations $\{r_0, r_{1/12}, r_{2/12}, \ldots\}$, the conditional distribution is Gaussian (exact transition density):

$$r_{t+\Delta t} \mid r_t \sim \mathcal{N}(\mu_t, v^2)$$

where:
$$\mu_t = r_t\,e^{-\kappa\Delta t} + \theta(1-e^{-\kappa\Delta t}), \qquad v^2 = \frac{\sigma^2(1-e^{-2\kappa\Delta t})}{2\kappa}$$

**The conditional mean is linear in $r_t$**, so OLS regression of $r_{t+1}$ on $r_t$ gives exact MLEs:

$$r_{t+\Delta t} = \underbrace{(1-e^{-\kappa\Delta t})\theta}_{a} + \underbrace{e^{-\kappa\Delta t}}_{b}\,r_t + \varepsilon_t, \qquad \varepsilon_t \sim \mathcal{N}(0, v^2)$$

Recover parameters from regression coefficients $(\hat{a}, \hat{b})$ and residual variance $\hat{s}^2$:
$$\hat{\kappa} = -\frac{\ln\hat{b}}{\Delta t}, \qquad \hat{\theta} = \frac{\hat{a}}{1-\hat{b}}, \qquad \hat{\sigma} = \hat{s}\sqrt{\frac{2\hat{\kappa}}{1-e^{-2\hat{\kappa}\Delta t}}}$$

```python
import numpy as np
import pandas as pd
from fredapi import Fred
import os

def calibrate_vasicek(rates: np.ndarray, dt: float = 1/12):
    """
    OLS calibration of Vasicek parameters from a monthly rate time series.
    
    Parameters
    ----------
    rates : np.ndarray
        Monthly short-rate observations (as decimals, e.g., 0.05 for 5%).
    dt : float
        Time step in years (1/12 for monthly data).
    
    Returns
    -------
    kappa, theta, sigma : floats
    """
    r_t  = rates[:-1]
    r_t1 = rates[1:]

    # OLS: r_{t+1} = a + b * r_t
    X      = np.column_stack([np.ones_like(r_t), r_t])
    coeffs, residuals, _, _ = np.linalg.lstsq(X, r_t1, rcond=None)
    a, b   = coeffs[0], coeffs[1]

    eps    = r_t1 - (a + b * r_t)
    s2     = np.var(eps, ddof=2)

    kappa  = -np.log(b) / dt
    theta  = a / (1 - b)
    sigma  = np.sqrt(s2 * 2 * kappa / (1 - np.exp(-2 * kappa * dt)))

    return kappa, theta, sigma


def load_and_calibrate(start='2000-01-01', end='2024-12-31'):
    """Fetch 1Y Treasury from FRED, convert to decimal, calibrate Vasicek."""
    fred = Fred(api_key=os.environ['FRED_API_KEY'])
    gs1  = fred.get_series('GS1', start=start, end=end).dropna()
    gs1  = gs1.resample('MS').last()          # monthly, start of month
    rates = gs1.values / 100.0                 # convert pct → decimal

    kappa, theta, sigma = calibrate_vasicek(rates)
    r0 = rates[-1]                             # current short rate

    print(f"κ (mean-reversion speed):  {kappa:.4f}  [yr⁻¹]")
    print(f"θ (long-run mean):         {theta*100:.2f}%")
    print(f"σ (volatility):            {sigma*100:.3f}%  [yr⁻¹/²]")
    print(f"r₀ (current rate):         {r0*100:.2f}%")
    print(f"Rate half-life:            {np.log(2)/kappa:.1f} years")
    return kappa, theta, sigma, r0
```

### 7.4 Simulation

```python
def simulate_vasicek(r0, kappa, theta, sigma, n_paths, n_steps, dt=1/12, seed=42):
    """
    Exact Monte Carlo simulation of the Vasicek short rate.
    
    Parameters
    ----------
    r0 : float         Current short rate (decimal)
    kappa : float      Mean-reversion speed
    theta : float      Long-run mean
    sigma : float      Volatility
    n_paths : int      Number of Monte Carlo paths
    n_steps : int      Number of monthly time steps
    dt : float         Time step (years); default 1/12
    seed : int         RNG seed for reproducibility
    
    Returns
    -------
    r : np.ndarray of shape (n_paths, n_steps + 1)
        r[:, 0] = r0 (initial condition)
        r[:, t] = short rate at month t
    """
    rng  = np.random.default_rng(seed)
    r    = np.zeros((n_paths, n_steps + 1))
    r[:, 0] = r0

    # Exact transition parameters (constant across steps for Vasicek)
    exp_kdt  = np.exp(-kappa * dt)
    mean_adj = theta * (1 - exp_kdt)
    vol_adj  = sigma * np.sqrt((1 - np.exp(-2*kappa*dt)) / (2*kappa))

    Z = rng.standard_normal((n_paths, n_steps))
    for t in range(n_steps):
        r[:, t+1] = r[:, t] * exp_kdt + mean_adj + vol_adj * Z[:, t]

    return r  # shape (n_paths, n_steps+1)
```

### 7.5 Bond Pricing and Yield Curve

```python
def vasicek_bond_price(r_t, kappa, theta, sigma, tau):
    """
    Closed-form Vasicek zero-coupon bond price.
    
    Parameters
    ----------
    r_t : float or np.ndarray   Current short rate(s)
    kappa, theta, sigma : float  Calibrated parameters
    tau : float                  Time to maturity (years)
    
    Returns
    -------
    P : same shape as r_t
    """
    B = (1 - np.exp(-kappa * tau)) / kappa
    A = np.exp(
        (theta - sigma**2 / (2 * kappa**2)) * (B - tau)
        - sigma**2 / (4 * kappa) * B**2
    )
    return A * np.exp(-B * r_t)


def vasicek_yield(r_t, kappa, theta, sigma, tau):
    """Continuously compounded yield for maturity tau."""
    P = vasicek_bond_price(r_t, kappa, theta, sigma, tau)
    return -np.log(P) / tau


def vasicek_yield_curve(r_t, kappa, theta, sigma,
                        maturities=(0.25, 0.5, 1, 2, 5, 10, 20, 30)):
    """Return a DataFrame of yields at standard maturities."""
    maturities = np.array(maturities)
    yields = vasicek_yield(r_t, kappa, theta, sigma, maturities)
    return pd.DataFrame({'maturity_yrs': maturities, 'yield': yields})


def mortgage_rate_from_short(r_paths, kappa, theta, sigma,
                             tau=30.0, mbs_spread=0.015):
    """
    Convert short rate paths to 30-year mortgage rate paths.
    
    mbs_spread : float
        Typical MBS-Treasury spread (~150bp). This captures the premium
        above the 30Y Treasury rate that mortgage borrowers pay.
    
    Returns
    -------
    mortgage_rates : same shape as r_paths
    """
    zcb_yield = vasicek_yield(r_paths, kappa, theta, sigma, tau)
    return zcb_yield + mbs_spread
```

### 7.6 Model Limitations

- **Negative rates:** $r_t$ is Gaussian and can go negative. For post-2022 calibrations to US data, this is rare but possible in simulation tails. Clip negative rates at zero in cash flow calculations: `r = np.maximum(r, 0)`.
- **Single-factor:** One Brownian motion drives all yields perfectly correlated. Real yield curves exhibit curve reshaping (twist, butterfly) that single-factor models cannot produce. This is acceptable for Level-1 MBS risk; add Hull-White or a two-factor model for more precision.
- **Constant parameters:** $\kappa$, $\theta$, $\sigma$ are constant. The Hull-White model makes $\theta$ time-dependent to fit the current yield curve exactly — a natural upgrade if you want to price relative to the market term structure rather than historical dynamics.
- **Physical vs. risk-neutral:** MLE calibration gives parameters under $\mathbb{P}$ (physical/historical measure). For risk-neutral pricing (OAS), you need to account for the market price of risk $\lambda$, which maps $\mathbb{P}$ to $\mathbb{Q}$: $dr_t = \kappa(\theta - r_t)dt + \sigma dW_t^{\mathbb{P}}$ becomes $dr_t = \kappa(\theta^* - r_t)dt + \sigma dW_t^{\mathbb{Q}}$ where $\theta^* = \theta - \lambda\sigma/\kappa$. For this project, use $\lambda = 0$ (simplest assumption); for production, calibrate $\lambda$ to match current bond prices.

## 8. Module D — Monte Carlo MBS Pricer

This is the integration layer. It ties all three risk modules together into a complete MBS valuation system.

### 8.1 Architecture

```
FRED Treasury Yields (GS1, GS30)
           │
           ▼
   Vasicek Calibration
   (κ, θ, σ, r₀)
           │
           ▼
   N Monte Carlo Paths  r(t, ω),  t = 0,...,360,  ω = 1,...,N
           │
     ┌─────┴──────┐
     │            │
     ▼            ▼
Mortgage Rate   Discount Factor
r^{30Y}(t,ω)   B(0,t,ω) = exp(-Σ r(s,ω)·Δt)
     │
     ▼
Refinancing Incentive
RI(t,ω) = coupon - r^{30Y}(t,ω)
     │
     ▼
Rate-Dependent CPR(t,ω)
     │
     ▼
SMM(t,ω) = 1 - (1-CPR)^{1/12}
     │
     ▼
Cash Flow Generator  CF(t,ω)
(Interest + Scheduled P + Prepayment)
     │
     ▼
PV(ω) = Σ_t  CF(t,ω) / B(0,t,ω)
     │
     ▼
MBS Price  P̂ = (1/N) Σ_ω PV(ω)
     │
     ▼
OAS: solve P_market = (1/N) Σ_ω Σ_t CF(t,ω) · e^{-λt} / B(0,t,ω)
```

### 8.2 Full Implementation

```python
import numpy as np
import pandas as pd
from scipy.optimize import brentq

# ── Pool Parameters ──────────────────────────────────────────────────
COUPON     = 0.065        # Weighted-average coupon of the pool (6.5%)
ORIG_UPB   = 1_000_000.0  # Original pool balance ($1M for illustration)
TERM       = 360          # 30-year FRM
MBS_SPREAD = 0.015        # MBS-Treasury spread added to 30Y Vasicek yield

# ── Simulation Parameters ────────────────────────────────────────────
N_PATHS    = 5_000
N_STEPS    = TERM         # monthly, so 360 steps
DT         = 1 / 12      # 1 month in years

# ── Vasicek Parameters (from calibration) ────────────────────────────
KAPPA = 0.15
THETA = 0.045
SIGMA = 0.012
R0    = 0.045


# ── Step 1: Simulate rate paths ──────────────────────────────────────
rate_paths = simulate_vasicek(R0, KAPPA, THETA, SIGMA, N_PATHS, N_STEPS, DT)
# shape: (N_PATHS, N_STEPS + 1)

# ── Step 2: Compute mortgage rate paths ──────────────────────────────
mortgage_paths = mortgage_rate_from_short(
    rate_paths[:, :-1], KAPPA, THETA, SIGMA, tau=30.0, mbs_spread=MBS_SPREAD
)
# shape: (N_PATHS, N_STEPS)

# ── Step 3: Compute rate-dependent CPR paths ─────────────────────────
cpr_paths = compute_cpr_paths(mortgage_paths, COUPON, TERM)
smm_paths = 1 - (1 - cpr_paths)**(1/12)
# shape: (N_PATHS, N_STEPS)

# ── Step 4: Generate cash flows ───────────────────────────────────────
def generate_cashflows(smm_paths, coupon, orig_upb, term):
    """
    Simulate monthly cash flows for a fixed-rate mortgage pool.
    
    Returns
    -------
    cashflows : np.ndarray of shape (n_paths, term)
    balances  : np.ndarray of shape (n_paths, term + 1)
    """
    n_paths, n_steps = smm_paths.shape
    monthly_rate = coupon / 12

    bal = np.zeros((n_paths, n_steps + 1))
    cf  = np.zeros((n_paths, n_steps))
    bal[:, 0] = orig_upb

    for t in range(n_steps):
        B   = bal[:, t]
        rem = term - t

        if rem <= 0:
            cf[:, t] = B          # remaining balance returned
            bal[:, t+1] = 0
            continue

        # Scheduled payment (annuity formula on current balance and remaining term)
        pmt        = B * (monthly_rate * (1+monthly_rate)**rem) / ((1+monthly_rate)**rem - 1)
        interest   = B * monthly_rate
        sched_prin = np.minimum(pmt - interest, B)

        # Unscheduled prepayment
        bal_after_sp = B - sched_prin
        prepay = bal_after_sp * smm_paths[:, t]

        cf[:, t]      = interest + sched_prin + prepay
        bal[:, t+1]   = np.maximum(bal_after_sp - prepay, 0.0)

    return cf, bal


cashflows, balances = generate_cashflows(smm_paths, COUPON, ORIG_UPB, TERM)

# ── Step 5: Stochastic discount factors ─────────────────────────────
def compute_discount_factors(rate_paths, dt=1/12):
    """
    B(0,t,ω) = exp(-∫₀ᵗ r(s,ω) ds) ≈ exp(-Σₛ₌₀ᵗ⁻¹ r(s,ω)·Δt)
    
    Returns
    -------
    disc : np.ndarray of shape (n_paths, n_steps)
         disc[:, t] = discount factor from t=0 to t=t+1
    """
    # Cumulative sum of rates × dt, shifted by 1 (discount at END of period)
    cum = np.cumsum(rate_paths[:, :-1] * dt, axis=1)
    return np.exp(-cum)

disc = compute_discount_factors(rate_paths, DT)
# shape: (N_PATHS, N_STEPS)

# ── Step 6: Price ────────────────────────────────────────────────────
pv_per_path = np.sum(cashflows * disc, axis=1)    # shape (N_PATHS,)
mbs_price   = np.mean(pv_per_path)
price_pct   = mbs_price / ORIG_UPB * 100

print(f"MBS Model Price:      ${mbs_price:>12,.2f}")
print(f"Price as % of par:    {price_pct:.4f}")
print(f"Std dev (across paths): {np.std(pv_per_path)/ORIG_UPB*100:.4f}%")

# ── Step 7: OAS Solver ───────────────────────────────────────────────
def price_at_oas(oas_lambda, cashflows, disc, dt=1/12):
    """PV of cash flows when OAS spread λ is added to the discount rate."""
    n_steps  = cashflows.shape[1]
    times    = np.arange(1, n_steps + 1) * dt                   # in years
    oas_disc = np.exp(-oas_lambda * times)[np.newaxis, :]        # (1, n_steps)
    pv       = np.sum(cashflows * disc * oas_disc, axis=1)       # (n_paths,)
    return np.mean(pv)


def solve_oas(market_price, cashflows, disc, dt=1/12, bracket=(-0.05, 0.25)):
    """
    Find OAS λ such that model price equals market_price.
    
    Parameters
    ----------
    market_price : float   Observed market price (e.g., $980,000 for 98 cents on dollar)
    
    Returns
    -------
    oas : float   OAS in decimal (multiply by 10,000 for bps)
    """
    f = lambda lam: price_at_oas(lam, cashflows, disc, dt) - market_price
    return brentq(f, *bracket, xtol=1e-8, maxiter=200)


market_price = ORIG_UPB * 0.98   # assume trades at 98 cents on dollar
oas = solve_oas(market_price, cashflows, disc)
print(f"\nOAS: {oas*10_000:.1f} bps")
```

## 9. OAS and Risk Metrics

### 9.1 OAS — Definition and Interpretation

**OAS (Option-Adjusted Spread)** is the constant spread $\lambda$ that, when added to the risk-free discount rate on every path and at every time step, makes the model price equal to the observed market price:

$$P_{\text{market}} = \frac{1}{N}\sum_{\omega=1}^N \sum_{t=1}^T \frac{CF(t,\omega)}{B(0,t,\omega) \cdot e^{\lambda t \cdot \Delta t}}$$

**Spread hierarchy:**

| Spread | What it strips out | What remains |
|---|---|---|
| **Nominal spread** (yield minus 5Y Treasury) | Nothing — confounds everything | Curve + option + credit |
| **Z-spread** | Term structure shape | Option + credit |
| **OAS** | Term structure + option cost | Credit + liquidity |

For an agency MBS (no credit risk), OAS ≈ liquidity premium. For a non-agency MBS, OAS includes credit compensation after paying for optionality.

$$\text{OAS} = \text{Z-spread} - \text{Option Cost}$$

An MBS trading at OAS = 0 is fairly valued relative to the term structure and its prepayment option. Positive OAS = cheap (you are overcompensated). Negative OAS = rich.

### 9.2 Effective Duration and Convexity

Computed by finite differences through the full Monte Carlo — not analytically.

```python
def mbs_price_at_rate(r0_shift, kappa, theta, sigma, coupon, orig_upb, term,
                      n_paths=2000, seed=99):
    """
    Price the MBS with the initial short rate shifted by r0_shift.
    Used for finite-difference duration and convexity.
    """
    rp = simulate_vasicek(R0 + r0_shift, kappa, theta, sigma, n_paths, term, DT, seed)
    mp = mortgage_rate_from_short(rp[:, :-1], kappa, theta, sigma)
    cp = compute_cpr_paths(mp, coupon, term)
    sm = 1 - (1 - cp)**(1/12)
    cf, _ = generate_cashflows(sm, coupon, orig_upb, term)
    dc = compute_discount_factors(rp, DT)
    return np.mean(np.sum(cf * dc, axis=1))


dr   = 0.0025   # 25bp bump
P0   = mbs_price
P_up = mbs_price_at_rate(+dr, KAPPA, THETA, SIGMA, COUPON, ORIG_UPB, TERM)
P_dn = mbs_price_at_rate(-dr, KAPPA, THETA, SIGMA, COUPON, ORIG_UPB, TERM)

eff_dur  = (P_dn - P_up) / (2 * P0 * dr)
eff_conv = (P_up + P_dn - 2 * P0) / (P0 * dr**2)

print(f"Effective Duration:  {eff_dur:.3f} years")
print(f"Effective Convexity: {eff_conv:.3f}")
```

**Interpretation:**
- **Effective Duration ~3–7 years** is typical for agency MBS (much shorter than a 30-year Treasury with ~18-year duration, because prepayments return principal early).
- **Effective Convexity negative** confirms the MBS has negative convexity — price response is concave in rate: falls more than it rises for symmetric shocks, and rallies less than it falls as rates drop (prepayments cap the upside).

## 12. Concept Mastery Q&A

These are questions that come up in derivatives and structured products interviews. Each answer here is at the level of rigor the interviewer expects.

---

**Q: What is a pass-through MBS and how does money flow?**

A pass-through MBS pools individual mortgages and passes the monthly cash flows from borrowers — net of a servicing fee — directly to the MBS investors, pro-rata by holding. Each month: borrowers pay interest + scheduled principal + any voluntary prepayments → servicer collects and nets the servicing fee → Freddie Mac (as guarantor) removes credit risk and passes through → investors receive their pro-rata share. The guaranty means agency MBS investors bear no credit risk; they bear only prepayment and interest rate risk.

---

**Q: What is PSA and what are its limitations?**

PSA is a standardized prepayment speed convention. 100% PSA assumes CPR ramps linearly at 0.2% per month for 30 months (reaching 6%), then stays flat at 6%. A pool is quoted as "$k$% PSA," meaning it prepays at $k/100$ of this schedule.

Limitations: PSA is a static backward-looking scalar. It does not model how prepayments change as rates move. Two pools with identical PSA speeds but different coupons will behave very differently in a rate rally (the higher-coupon pool will prepay much faster as more borrowers can refinance). PSA cannot price optionality, compute OAS, or stress-test across rate scenarios. It is a quoting convention, not a risk model.

---

**Q: What is OAS and how does it differ from yield spread and Z-spread?**

- **Yield spread:** Difference between the MBS yield (under one PSA assumption) and a benchmark Treasury. Conflates term structure, optionality, and credit. Meaningless for comparing MBS with different prepayment speeds.
- **Z-spread:** Constant spread to the entire risk-free yield curve such that discounting projected cash flows (under one static prepayment scenario) gives the market price. Removes term structure effects but still ignores optionality.
- **OAS:** Constant spread to the risk-free rate across all Monte Carlo interest rate paths such that the *expected* present value of stochastic cash flows equals the market price. Removes term structure and option cost. For agency MBS: OAS ≈ liquidity premium. OAS = Z-spread − Option Cost. A negative OAS means the investor is paying more for the optionality than they are receiving in spread — the bond is expensive.

---

**Q: Why do MBS have negative convexity?**

Convexity measures the curvature of the price/yield relationship: $d^2P/dy^2$. A standard bond has positive convexity (price rises faster than it falls for symmetric rate moves — the curve smiles). MBS have negative convexity because the borrower holds a call option:

- **Rates fall:** Refinancing incentive increases → CPR rises → the investor gets principal back at par early and must reinvest at the new lower rates. The price appreciation you would expect from holding a long-duration bond is capped. The price/yield curve becomes concave (frowns) at low rates.
- **Rates rise:** Refinancing incentive falls → CPR slows → the bond's duration extends. You are holding a longer bond in a rising-rate environment — worst case.

Both directions work against the MBS investor. The portfolio DV01 ($=P \times D \times 0.0001$) increases when rates rise (duration extension) and decreases when rates fall (duration contraction) — exactly the wrong behavior for a hedger.

**Q: Explain the Vasicek model from first principles.**

Vasicek (1977) models the instantaneous risk-free rate $r_t$ as an Ornstein-Uhlenbeck process:
$$dr_t = \kappa(\theta - r_t)\,dt + \sigma\,dW_t$$

$\kappa$ is mean-reversion speed, $\theta$ is the long-run rate level, $\sigma$ is volatility, $W_t$ is Brownian motion. The drift term $\kappa(\theta - r_t)dt$ pulls $r_t$ back toward $\theta$ whenever it deviates. The diffusion $\sigma\,dW_t$ adds Gaussian noise.

Because the SDE is linear in $r_t$ with a deterministic integrating factor $e^{\kappa t}$, the exact solution is Gaussian: $r_t \mid r_0 \sim \mathcal{N}(r_0 e^{-\kappa t} + \theta(1-e^{-\kappa t}), \sigma^2(1-e^{-2\kappa t})/(2\kappa))$. This Gaussianity gives zero-coupon bond prices in closed form via $P(t,T) = A(t,T)\exp(-B(t,T)r_t)$, making the model fully analytically tractable. The drawback is that rates can go negative, which is the price of Gaussianity.

**Q: How do you calibrate Vasicek to data?**

Under the physical measure $\mathbb{P}$, the conditional distribution of $r_{t+\Delta t}$ given $r_t$ is Gaussian with mean $\mu = r_t e^{-\kappa\Delta t} + \theta(1-e^{-\kappa\Delta t})$ and variance $v^2 = \sigma^2(1-e^{-2\kappa\Delta t})/(2\kappa)$. The conditional mean is linear in $r_t$, so you fit by OLS regression of $r_{t+1}$ on $r_t$ to get intercept $\hat{a}$ and slope $\hat{b}$, then recover: $\hat{\kappa} = -\ln(\hat{b})/\Delta t$, $\hat{\theta} = \hat{a}/(1-\hat{b})$, $\hat{\sigma}$ from the residual variance. Data: monthly 1-Year Treasury yields from FRED (GS1), 2000–2024, converted to decimal.

**Q: What is the difference between the physical and risk-neutral measures in this context?**

The physical measure $\mathbb{P}$ describes how the short rate actually evolves historically (what you observe). Calibrating to historical data gives you $(\kappa, \theta, \sigma)$ under $\mathbb{P}$. For pricing derivatives, you need the risk-neutral measure $\mathbb{Q}$, under which discounted asset prices are martingales. Moving from $\mathbb{P}$ to $\mathbb{Q}$ involves a market price of risk $\lambda$: $dr_t = \kappa(\theta - r_t)dt + \sigma dW_t^{\mathbb{P}} = \kappa(\theta^* - r_t)dt + \sigma dW_t^{\mathbb{Q}}$ where $\theta^* = \theta - \lambda\sigma/\kappa$. For this project, setting $\lambda = 0$ (risk-neutral = physical) is a simplifying assumption. In practice, $\theta$ under $\mathbb{Q}$ should be calibrated to fit current bond prices, which gives a better match to the term structure for pricing.

**Q: Why is Cox PH more appropriate than logistic regression for mortgage default?**

Logistic regression treats every loan observation as either "defaulted" or "did not default." But a loan observed in December 2024 that has not yet defaulted is not a confirmed non-defaulter — it is **right-censored**: we know it has survived to the observation date but its future is unknown. Logistic regression ignores this and labels censored loans as "no default," which biases the coefficient estimates downward for origination-era covariates (since young loans simply haven't had time to default). Cox PH's partial likelihood conditions on the risk set at each event time: when a loan defaults at month $t$, it compares that loan's covariates against all loans still at risk at month $t$. Censored loans contribute correctly by being in the risk set up to their censoring time and then dropping out. Cox PH is the canonical method for time-to-event data with censoring.

**Q: What is the proportional hazards assumption and when might it fail for mortgages?**

The PH assumption says the hazard ratio between any two loans is constant over time: $h(t|\mathbf{x}_i)/h(t|\mathbf{x}_j) = \exp((\mathbf{x}_i-\mathbf{x}_j)^\top\boldsymbol{\beta})$, independent of $t$. This means covariate effects are multiplicative and time-invariant.

For mortgages, this can fail: FICO's effect is strong in the first few years (origination quality matters most when the loan is young) but may weaken as behavioral payment history accumulates. LTV's effect strengthens over time as a loan moves into or out of negative equity. These violations are tested via Schoenfeld residuals: if the scaled residuals for a covariate show a systematic trend with time, the PH assumption is violated for that variable. Fix: include time-interaction terms, or stratify the baseline hazard by the violating variable.

**Q: Walk me through the MBS pricing pipeline end to end.**

Starting with Vasicek parameters calibrated to 24 years of monthly 1Y Treasury yields via OLS: (1) Simulate 5,000 monthly short-rate paths over 30 years using the exact Euler-Maruyama transition. (2) Convert each path to a 30-year mortgage rate using the Vasicek closed-form bond pricing formula plus a constant MBS-Treasury spread. (3) For each path, compute the monthly refinancing incentive (pool coupon minus path mortgage rate) and feed into a logistic PSA speed function to get a path-dependent CPR. Convert CPR to SMM. (4) Simulate pool cash flows month by month: annuity formula gives scheduled payment; subtract interest to get scheduled principal; multiply surviving balance by SMM to get prepayment; sum for total cash flow. (5) Compute stochastic discount factors as the exponential of the cumulative short rate along each path. (6) Dot cash flows with discount factors and average across paths for the model price. (7) Find OAS by Brent's method: add a constant spread $\lambda$ to the discount rate until the model price matches the market price. (8) Bump the initial rate by ±25bp and reprice to get effective duration and convexity via symmetric finite differences.
